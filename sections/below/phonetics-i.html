2  Phonetics I

Today, we are going to discuss what makes human communication system special!

Spoilers
  • Arbitrariness, yes again! Language is consists of parts that are almost completely arbitrary. There is no natural sign-meaning mapping.
  • Displacement: We can talk about that is not immediately in front of us or that is not immediately happening!
  • Productivity: From the finite set of items, we can create infinite set of sentences that are almost immediately understood by the speaker!
  • Combinatorics: We carry out this productivity with an finite set of tools as well!
  • Sounds: Sounds is a great place to start to see what finite set of things can give rise to!

2.2 Sounds

Phonetics
  • Physiological, aerodynamic and acoustic characteristics of speech sounds.
  • As such really a branch of physics/biology.
  • Typical questions:
    1. What does our mouth do when we say Ian as opposed to Canada?
    2. What does the sound wave look like that is produced when we say Ian?
    3. Do the individual sounds of a word become shorter if the word as a whole becomes longer?
    4. What happens in our ears when we hear i as opposed to a?
    5. Why do we sound nasal when we have a cold?
Spelling (orthography) is not phonetics.
  • Spelling tries to give an accurate representation of sound impressions.
  • English (and other languages) often fails quite miserably at that.
  • Spelling is about letters, phonetics is about sound.
  • Phonology is about the system that actually underlies all pronunciation, i.e. a kind of knowledge in our brain.

Questions to think about - How many ways are there to spell the sound ‘i’ in English? - How many ways are there to pronounce the letter a in English? - French eau ‘water’ is pronounced - Why pterodactylus does not sound out everything but sounds out only [tɛɹədæktɪləs]?
